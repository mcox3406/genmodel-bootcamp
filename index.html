1Course premise

The course works from the current literature rather than its historical development. We assume participants know the high-level story of diffusion and flow matching, and use two weeks of compressed foundations to make current papers readable before moving into fast generation, discrete and hybrid spaces, guidance, Schrodinger bridges, molecules, proteins, crystals, and evaluation.

Five questions recur throughout. What is the state space, what dynamics are learned, what symmetries matter, what evaluator defines success, and what failure modes can fool us?

Where the learning happens. Most of the learning in this course happens while working through the derivations and coding labs, not while reading. A reasonable weekly rhythm is one pass through the readings for orientation, then most of your time on the problems, returning to the papers and notes when you get stuck.

2Structure of the course

The parts group the weeks by mathematical content rather than by publication date. Weeks 1–4 develop a single construction: a path of distributions from a tractable base to the data, together with a learned field that transports samples along it. Diffusion models, flow matching, and stochastic interpolants differ in how the path is specified and in which object is regressed, a score or a velocity, not in kind. Presenting diffusion as background and flow matching as its successor would obscure the fact that they are the same estimator applied to different paths.

Weeks 5–7 treat the choices that construction leaves open: the coupling between base and data samples, which sets how much paths cross and curve; the number of solver steps a sampler needs, which that geometry determines; and the state space itself, since molecules and crystals mix continuous coordinates with categorical types. Weeks 8–9 are two instances of one operation, conditioning a reference process. Guidance and reward alignment impose a constraint on the terminal distribution, a one-sided conditioning solved by a Doob h-transform or its approximations; Schrodinger bridges pin both endpoint marginals, the two-sided version. Both are naturally posed as stochastic control, which is why they sit together. Weeks 10–12 apply these tools to molecules, proteins, and crystals, and end with evaluation and failure-mode analysis.

  1. Learning the dynamicsWeeks 1–4. Construct a path of distributions from noise to data and learn the field that transports along it, written as a score, a velocity, or an interpolant.
  2. The design spaceWeeks 5–7. The choices that construction leaves open: the coupling and path geometry, the solver-step budget, and state spaces that mix continuous and discrete variables.
  3. Conditioning & controlWeeks 8–9. Conditioning a learned process: guidance and reward alignment constrain one endpoint, Schrodinger bridges constrain both.
  4. Applications & evaluationWeeks 10–12. Molecules, proteins, and crystals, and the evaluation and failure-mode analysis that separates useful structures from merely valid ones.
