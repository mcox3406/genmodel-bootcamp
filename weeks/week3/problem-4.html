Source note. Original lab. It is a deliberate miniature: the flat-torus fractional-coordinate geometry and mean-free translation-invariant construction of FlowMM (Miller et al., 2024), built on the geodesic conditional flow of Riemannian Flow Matching (Chen & Lipman, 2023), in the coordinates-only setting that strips away the lattice and atom types. Sanity checks in the spirit of Week 2 Problem 4.

Scope. A real crystal generator (FlowMM, CrystalFlow, Week 11) jointly flows the fractional coordinates, the lattice, and the atom types, three spaces with different geometries. This lab builds only the coordinate piece, the flat torus \([0,1)^{3n}\), because it is where the new idea lives: the interpolant is a geodesic, not a straight line, and translation invariance is periodic.

Data layout. Use \(d=2\) for visualization and \(n\ge2\) atoms per configuration, so a sample is a point cloud \(X\in\mathbb T^{n\times2}\), not a single point. Global translation invariance and the radial distribution function are only meaningful for multi-atom configurations. Build a few toy motifs whose atoms sit near opposite cell boundaries, perturb each site by a wrapped Gaussian \(\mathrm{to\_cell}(\text{site}+\sigma\,\text{noise})\), and optionally apply a random global shift. Use a half-open minimum-image convention so the measure-zero tie \(u=0.5\), where the shortest geodesic is not unique, is handled deterministically.

Problem 3 trained a flow where the state was an unstructured vector in \(\mathbb R^d\). A crystal's atomic positions are not unstructured: in fractional coordinates they live on a torus, because the unit cell is periodic and coordinate \(0.99\) is next to \(0.01\), not far from it. A straight Euclidean line between two fractional coordinates can take the long way around the cell, or leave \([0,1)\) entirely. This lab builds the smallest flow that respects the periodicity, a Riemannian flow matching whose conditional path is the toroidal geodesic, and shows exactly why the naive Euclidean flow fails. The point is to see that the interpolant, not just the network, must know about the state space, the Week 3 analogue of Week 2's equivariant denoiser.

Implement a small set of functions with clean interfaces.

wrap_disp(u)                           # minimum-image displacement into [-0.5, 0.5)
to_cell(x)                             # wrap coordinates into [0, 1)
geodesic(x0, x1, t)                    # x_t = to_cell(x0 + t * wrap_disp(x1 - x0))
velocity_target(x0, x1)                # wrap_disp(x1 - x0): geodesic conditional velocity
mean_free(delta)                       # delta - atomwise mean: the FlowMM tangent-space fix
cfm_loss_torus(model, x0, x1, t)       # || v_theta(x_t, t) - target ||^2
sample_torus_ode(model, x0, n_steps)   # integrate on the torus, wrapping each step
periodic_rdf(coords, cell)             # multi-atom RDF with minimum-image distances
translation_error(model, X, shift)     # equivariance check under a global cell shift
euclidean_baseline(...)                # same model, straight lines in R^d, no wrapping
  1. The torus and the geodesic interpolant. Build the multi-atom target described above: several motifs \(X_1\in\mathbb T^{n\times2}\) (Wyckoff-like positions, some atoms near the cell edges so wrapping matters), each site given a small wrapped Gaussian spread, as a stand-in for a crystal's coordinate distribution. Take the base distribution to be uniform on the torus (the natural periodic prior, not a Gaussian). Implement the geodesic \(x_t=\mathrm{to\_cell}\big(x_0+t\,\mathrm{wrap\_disp}(x_1-x_0)\big)\), where \(\mathrm{wrap\_disp}\) is the minimum-image displacement into \([-0.5,0.5)\), and show it is the shortest path on the flat torus. Confirm its conditional velocity is the constant \(\mathrm{wrap\_disp}(x_1-x_0)\), the manifold analogue of \(x_1-x_0\), so CFM applies verbatim with this target.
  2. Train the toroidal flow. Reuse the CFM loop of Problem 3 with two changes: form \(x_t\) by the geodesic, and regress \(v_\theta(x_t,t)\) onto \(\mathrm{wrap\_disp}(x_1-x_0)\). The velocity lives in the tangent space (for the flat torus, \(\mathbb R^2\) with periodic identification), but a raw MLP on raw coordinates is not periodic in \(x\), so it will not respect \(v_\theta(0,t)=v_\theta(1,t)\): either treat that as an intentional lab simplification, or feed periodic features \(\phi(x)=(\sin2\pi x,\cos2\pi x)\) (and, for a translation-invariant model, wrapped pairwise displacements). Integrate the ODE on the torus, wrapping into \([0,1)\) after each step, and confirm the generated coordinates reproduce the target site distribution (compare a periodic radial distribution function, using minimum-image distances, to the data).
  3. The Euclidean baseline fails. Now train the identical model with straight lines and no wrapping: interpolate \(x_t=(1-t)x_0+t x_1\) in \(\mathbb R^2\), regress onto \(x_1-x_0\), and sample without wrapping. Be precise about the guaranteed failure: it is topological, not leakage. Euclidean chords ignore the identification \(0\sim1\), so they take the long way through the cell interior instead of the short wrapped path across the boundary, which distorts the density near periodic boundaries and splits or smears boundary-adjacent sites. Quantify it with the path-inefficiency ratio \(\|x_1-x_0\|/\|\mathrm{wrap\_disp}(x_1-x_0)\|\), the boundary-region density error, and the periodic-RDF (or wrapped energy-distance) error against the data. An out-of-cell fraction is only meaningful if you deliberately run the baseline in \(\mathbb R^2\) without projection: with both endpoints inside \([0,1)^2\) the straight interpolant stays in the cube during training, so leakage is a model extrapolation artifact, not a necessary failure. This is the coordinate-space version of the warning at the end of Week 2 Problem 4.
  4. Periodic translation invariance. A crystal's coordinates are only defined up to a global shift of the origin: translating every atom by the same fractional vector (mod 1) is the same crystal. Get the reason right: on the compact torus a translation-invariant density is perfectly normalizable (the uniform density is one), so the issue is not non-normalizability but a gauge redundancy, many fractional-coordinate representatives describe the same crystal, so the model should either work on the quotient by global shifts or fix a gauge. Implement FlowMM's fix, which is a mean-free tangent velocity, not a coordinate mean: an ordinary Euclidean mean does not transfer to the torus, where the mean is branch-dependent and non-unique. With \(\Delta_i=\mathrm{wrap\_disp}(x_{1,i}-x_{0,i})\) and \(\bar\Delta=\tfrac1n\sum_i\Delta_i\), train on the mean-free target \(u_i^{\mathrm{mf}}=\Delta_i-\bar\Delta\) and integrate \(x_t=\mathrm{to\_cell}(x_0+t\,u^{\mathrm{mf}})\); at \(t=1\) the path reaches a globally shifted copy of \(x_1\), the same equivalence class rather than the original representative. Verify with translation_error that a global cell shift of the input produces the correspondingly shifted output (translation-equivariance), and that any shift-invariant observable of the generated crystal is unchanged.
  5. Geodesic straightness and few-step sampling. Repeat the straightness and NFE experiments of Problem 3 on the torus: geodesic conditional paths are the manifold "straight lines," so the toroidal flow should again sample well in few steps, while the Euclidean baseline needs more steps and still misses the boundary. Report the few-step comparison and the wrapped-distance energy metric, and note that a minibatch OT coupling on the torus (using wrapped distances as the cost) would straighten further, the FoldFlow-OT idea on a different manifold.
  6. Toward the real models. Write a short paragraph on two extensions you did not implement. (a) The full crystal: FlowMM flows the lattice (a constrained matrix, Niggli-reduced to fix the basis gauge) and the atom types (categorical, needing a discrete or relaxed flow, Week 7) jointly with the coordinates; explain why their differing geometries make the relative "speed" of the three flows a design decision, the periodic echo of the scale-mismatch issue in Week 2 Problem 2. (b) Protein frames: FoldFlow runs the same construction on \(SE(3)=SO(3)\times\mathbb R^3\), where the geodesic on \(SO(3)\) uses the matrix exponential and logarithm; state what changes (a non-abelian group, curvature, the tangent space is no longer trivial) and what stays the same (geodesic conditional path, CFM verbatim). Weeks 10 and 11 are these in full.
Deliverable. Why this matters. This is the smallest complete instance of the idea that dominates generative modeling for materials and proteins: the flow must live on the state space, and the interpolant is where the geometry enters. Once you have built a geodesic flow on the torus and watched the Euclidean baseline break at the boundary, FlowMM, CrystalFlow, and FoldFlow read as the same construction on richer manifolds, exactly as the equivariant denoiser of Week 2 opened up the diffusion models.
