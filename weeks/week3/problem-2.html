Straightness, path curvature, and rectified flow

Why straight conditional paths still give a curved flow, and how reflow straightens it without moving the marginals, the mechanism that turns flow matching into few-step generation.

Source note. Original derivation following Liu, Gong & Liu (2022), §2, with the transport-cost framing of Tong et al. (2023) and the sampling-cost thread of Lipman et al. (2022), §6.2.

Problem 1 gave you a velocity you can train. This problem is about what makes it fast. A trained flow is only as cheap to sample as its paths are easy to integrate, and the paths are easy exactly when they are straight: a perfectly straight trajectory is integrated with a single Euler step and zero error. The catch, and the whole subtlety of the week, is that choosing straight conditional interpolants does not make the marginal flow straight. This problem quantifies the gap and derives the fix, rectified flow's reflow, proving that it preserves the marginals while reducing transport cost.

Two straightnesses. Keep them apart. A conditional path \(x_t=(1-t)x_0+t x_1\) is a straight line by construction, for a fixed endpoint pair. The marginal trajectory is the path an actual sample follows under the learned ODE \(\dot x=u_t(x)\); it is generally curved, because at each point the velocity is an average over many endpoint pairs. Straightness is a property of the marginal flow, and it is what few-step sampling needs.

Idealized setting. Assume \(p_0,q\) have finite second moments, all relevant conditional expectations exist, and the velocity is the exact population minimizer of the CFM objective so the induced ODE has unique solutions; unless a part says otherwise, integration is exact too. Statements about trajectories hold \(p_0\)-almost surely (the functionals below only constrain trajectories on the support of the sampled flow). In Parts 3-4, "transport cost" means the endpoint coupling cost \(T(\pi)=\mathbb E_\pi\|x_1-x_0\|^2\); keep it distinct from the kinetic energy of the marginal vector field.

  1. Straightness and one-step error. Write \(Z_t\) for the ODE trajectory the velocity \(v\) generates from \(Z_0\sim p_0\) (kept distinct from the interpolant \(x_t=(1-t)x_0+t x_1\) used elsewhere), and define the trajectory straightness functional \[ S(Z)=\int_0^1\mathbb E\big\|(Z_1-Z_0)-\dot Z_t\big\|^2\,\mathrm dt, \] the time-averaged squared deviation of the instantaneous velocity \(\dot Z_t=v(Z_t,t)\) from the straight chord direction. Show \(S(Z)=0\) if and only if \(p_0\)-almost every trajectory is a straight line traversed at constant speed (the functional constrains only trajectories on the support of the sampled flow), and that in this case a single Euler step \(Z_1=Z_0+v(Z_0,0)\) is exact. Then bound the one-step path error by curvature alone, \(\big\|Z_1-(Z_0+v(Z_0,0))\big\|\le\int_0^1(1-t)\|\ddot Z_t\|\,\mathrm dt\). For the \(N\)-step Euler method, note that curvature is not enough on its own: assume in addition that \(v\) is \(L\)-Lipschitz and \(\|\ddot Z_t\|\le M\), and derive the standard \(O(M/N)\) global error bound with the \(e^{L}\)-type stability factor that accounts for propagated local errors, so that "straight" and "cheap to sample" are the same statement. (This trajectory straightness is distinct from, though aligned with, the straightness error \(\widehat S\), the CFM training residual on coupled interpolant points, that Problem 3 measures.)
  2. Straight conditional, curved marginal. Show by explicit example that straight conditional paths give a curved marginal flow. Take the concrete 1D case \(x_0\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)\), \(x_1\in\{-a,+a\}\) each with probability \(\tfrac12\) and independent of \(x_0\), and \(x_t=(1-t)x_0+t x_1\). Using \(u_t(x)=\mathbb E[x_1-x_0\mid x_t=x]\) from Problem 1, derive \[ u_t(x)=\frac{a\tanh\!\big(\tfrac{t a x}{(1-t)^2\sigma^2}\big)-x}{1-t}, \] and evaluate the acceleration \(\partial_t u_t(x)+u_t(x)\,\partial_x u_t(x)\) at one concrete \((t,x)\) to exhibit that it is nonzero, so the marginal trajectory bends. Explain the mechanism: independent-coupling conditional lines cross, but the marginal ODE is deterministic and its trajectories cannot cross, so the flow must curve to route around the crossings. Conclude that curvature is a property of the coupling, not of the interpolant.
  3. Transport cost. Define the transport cost of a coupling \(\pi\), \(\;T(\pi)=\mathbb E_{(x_0,x_1)\sim\pi}\|x_1-x_0\|^2\), which is the kinetic action of the noncausal straight interpolation \(x_t=(1-t)x_0+t x_1\). Distinguish it from the kinetic energy of the causal marginal ODE field, and prove by Jensen that the latter is never larger, \(\int_0^1\mathbb E\|u_t(x_t)\|^2\mathrm dt\le\mathbb E\|x_1-x_0\|^2\). Show that among all couplings with the fixed marginals \(p_0,q\), the transport cost is minimized by the quadratic optimal-transport (Monge) coupling, whose displacement interpolation is straight and non-crossing (Benamou-Brenier). This is the ideal that the reflow of part 4 and the minibatch OT of Problem 3 both chase. Be careful with the takeaway: lower transport cost and straighter paths are aligned goals here, but not equivalent in general, in \(d>1\) a straight coupling need not be quadratic-cost optimal.
  4. The reflow operator. Let \(v^{(0)}\) be a trained flow from the independent coupling \(\pi^{(0)}=p_0\otimes q\), with flow map \(G^{(0)}\) sending \(x_0\mapsto x_1\). Define the reflow coupling \(\pi^{(1)}=\mathrm{law}\big(x_0,G^{(0)}(x_0)\big)\) and train a new flow \(v^{(1)}\) by CFM on the straight lines of \(\pi^{(1)}\). Prove the two defining properties: (a) marginal preservation, \(\pi^{(1)}\) has the same marginals \(p_0,q\) as \(\pi^{(0)}\) (so reflow does not change the distribution being generated); and (b) transport-cost non-increase, \(T(\pi^{(1)})\le T(\pi^{(0)})\), and more generally non-increase of any convex transport cost. For (b), determinism of the new coupling is not itself enough (a deterministic map can have large cost): use marginal preservation \(z_t\stackrel{d}{=}x_t\) along the rectified flow, the identity \(v_t(x)=\mathbb E[x_1-x_0\mid x_t=x]\), and Jensen's inequality applied to the convex cost. State why exact reflow reaches a deterministic coupling in one step, so iterating it drives the flow toward a straight, non-crossing fixed point rather than merely toward determinism.
  5. Reflow does not hurt the marginals, in practice. The clean theory of part 4 assumes exact training and integration. Explain the two error sources that make real reflow imperfect: the flow map \(G^{(0)}\) is approximate, and each reflow round regresses on samples from the previous (approximate) flow, so errors can compound. State the standard tradeoff, one or two reflow rounds straighten paths a lot while distillation for genuine one-step sampling (Week 6) trades a further quality drop for speed, and relate this to the deterministic-versus-stochastic cost discussion of Week 2 Problem 2.
  6. Why flow matching scales. Collect the reasons the objective is well-behaved at scale, and be precise about each. (a) Simulation-free: the CFM loss of Problem 1 is a regression with no ODE solve, no divergence, and no likelihood in the loop, unlike the adjoint training of a continuous normalizing flow. (b) Well-conditioned target: the regression target is a finite-variance, order-one velocity (not bounded, but with no explicit low-noise \(1/\sigma_t\) amplification of the kind that appears in score parameterizations), so the loss stays order-one across \(t\) without the elaborate EDM preconditioning of Week 2 (compare the two targets near \(t=1\)). (c) Path freedom: straighter paths need fewer function evaluations at sampling time. Point to the empirical evidence: SiT is the clean controlled case, beating DiT at matched backbone, parameter count, and GFLOPs; Stable Diffusion 3 compares rectified-flow against diffusion formulations at scale but also changes architecture and training, so cite its controlled ablation rather than "the same transformer." State which of (a)-(c) you think is doing the work, and how you would test it.
  7. Curvature you can measure. Give two operational curvature diagnostics you will use in Problem 3: (i) the straightness error \(\widehat S=\mathbb E_{(x_0,x_1),t}\|v_\theta(x_t,t)-(x_1-x_0)\|^2\), the CFM residual on coupled interpolant points (and, optionally, the trajectory straightness \(S(Z)\) estimated by Monte Carlo along sampled ODE trajectories), and (ii) the gap between a \(1\)-step and a many-step Euler sample of the same initial noise. Argue that both should fall under OT coupling and under reflow, and predict their qualitative shape as a function of the number of function evaluations. This sets up the experiments.
Why this matters. Straightness is what separates "flow matching works" from "flow matching is fast." The reflow argument is the first appearance of the finite-time-flow-map idea that Week 6 turns into one-step and few-step generation, and the transport-cost view leads directly to the couplings and optimal transport of Week 5. Getting the conditional-versus-marginal straightness distinction right is what keeps you from expecting a straight interpolant to give a straight sampler for free.
